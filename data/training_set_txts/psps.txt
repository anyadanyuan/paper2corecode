# 1 Introduction

Leveraging machine learning (ML) techniques to enhance and accelerate research has become increasingly popular in many scientific disciplines (Wang et al., 2023) . For example, sophisticated deep learning frameworks have achieved remarkable success in predicting protein structure and interactions, which has the potential to significantly speed up the research process, save costs, and revolutionize the field of structural biology (Jumper et al., 2021; Abramson et al., 2024; Ahdritz et al., 2024) . However, several recent studies have pointed out that statistical inference using ML-predicted outcomes may lead to invalid scientific discoveries due to the lack of consideration of ML prediction uncertainty in traditional statistical approaches (Wang et al., 2020; Angelopoulos et al., 2023a; Miao et al., 2023b) . To address this, researchers have introduced methods that couple ML prediction on large samples and a small set of individuals with measured outcome data to ensure the validity of ML-assisted statistical inference (Wang et al., 2020; Angelopoulos et al., 2023a; Miao et al., 2023b) .
Despite these advances, current ML-assisted inference methods can only address basic statistical tasks, including mean estimation, quantile estimation, and linear and logistic regression (Angelopoulos et al., 2023a; Miao et al., 2023b) . While the same mathematical principle behind existing ML-assisted inference methods can be generalized to a broader class of M-estimation problems, specific algebraic derivations and computational implementations are required for each new statistical task. Moreover, many tasks, such as the Wilcoxon rank-sum test, do not fit into the M-estimation framework. These issues pose significant challenges to the broad application of ML-assisted inference across various scientific domains. more powerful compared to classical methods.
We first provide some intuition about ML-assisted inference and our proposed protocol using an example.

## 2.3 Related work

Our work is closely related to recent methods developed in the ML-assisted inference literature (Angelopoulos et al., 2023a; Miao et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2020; Angelopoulos et al., 2023b; Gan and Liang, 2023; Motwani and Witten, 2023; Zrnic and Candès, 2024) , and is also related to methods for handling missing data (Rubin, 1996; Tsiatis, 2006) and semi-supervised inference (Azriel et al., 2022; Zhang and Bradic, 2022; Deng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2019) . While current ML-assisted inference methods modify the loss function or the estimating equation, our protocol works directly on the summary statistics. For simple problems such as mean estimation, current methods yield a closed-form solution to the optimization problem. However, for more general statistical tasks, there is no such closed-form solution. Current methods typically require the algebraic form of the loss function, its first and second order derivatives, and the standard error for the estimator, as well as a newly implemented optimization algorithm to obtain the estimator. For each new statistical task, as long as an existing analysis routine can produce an estimator that is asymptotically normally distributed, our protocol can be applied in a similar manner. Additionally, the current mathematical principles guiding ML-assisted inference are applicable solely to M-estimation (Angelopoulos et al., 2023a; Miao et al., 2023b; Angelopoulos et al., 2023b; Gan and Liang, 2023; Zrnic and Candès, 2024) . However, our protocol extends beyond this limitation, addressing all estimation problems with an asymptotically normally distributed estimator.
Inference relying solely on summary statistics is widely used in the statistical genetics literature for practical reasons. Summary statistics-based methods have been developed for tasks such as variance component inference and polygenic risk prediction (Bulik-Sullivan et al., 2015b,a; Ruan et al., 2022; Miao et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2022) . In contrast to our work, these applications do not leverage ML predictions, but instead focus on inference using summary statistics obtained from observed outcomes. An exception is our previous study for valid genome-wide association studies on ML-predicted outcome (Miao et al., 2024) . But our previous work focused only on linear regression modeling with application to genome-wide association studies. The PSPS introduced in this paper aims to extend ML-assisted inference to general statistical tasks.
Our work is also related to resampling-based inference, zero augmentation, and false discovery rate (FDR) control methods. Our protocol is inspired by the core principle of resampling-based inference, which replaces algebraic derivation with computation (Efron and Tibshirani, 1994) . The main difference is that we focus on how to use ML to support inference, whereas resampling-based inference focuses on bias and variance estimation. The idea of zero augmentation has been used in augmented inverse propensity weighting estimators (Robins et al., 1994) and in handling unmeasured confounders (Yang and Ding, 2019) and missing data for U-statistics (Cannings and Fan, 2022) . These estimators do not incorporate ML, which is fundamental to our work. We also adapt techniques from the FDR literature (Benjamini and Hochberg, 1995; Benjamini and Yekutieli, 2001; Barber and Candès, 2015, 2019) . Our unique contribution is to use ML to support FDR control, thereby increasing its statistical power, in contrast to classical methods that rely solely on labeled data.

# 4 ML-assisted False Discovery Rate Control

Controlling FDR is crucial for statistical application in various scientific disciplines (Benjamini and Hochberg, 1995; Benjamini and Yekutieli, 2001; Barber and Candès, 2015) . In this section, we show that our protocol can be utilized for ML-assisted FDR control, achieving greater statistical power compared to classical methods that solely rely on labeled data. We consider the linear regression model with
Y = K k=1 X k β k + ϵ, ϵ ∼ N 0, σ 2 I n
Here, Y is a n-dimensional vector, X k is also a n-dimensional vector representing the k-th feature. A central question here is to select the variables associated with the outcome Y after accounting for all other variables. Formally, denote S ⊂ {1, 2, . . . , K} as the set of selected variables. The FDR is defined as
FDR = E[ # k : β k = 0, k ∈ S #{k : k ∈ S} ∨ 1 ].
We consider two popular FDR control methods: Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) procedure (Benjamini and Hochberg, 1995) and knockoff (Barber and Candès, 2015) . BH procedure first computes p-values p k for each feature X k using multiple linear regression and then applies a threshold to these p-values. Features with p-values smaller than the threshold are considered as discoveries. The knockoff method does not rely on p-values. It includes three key components: a ranking algorithm that assigns an importance metric to each variable, an augmented design that creates fake variables, and a symmetric statistic that utilizes the importance metric for each original variable to control the final FDR (Ke et al., 2024) .
We introduce PSPS-BH and PSPS-knockoff for ML-assisted FDR control. Briefly, PSPS-BH processes the p-value from ML-assisted linear regression through the BH procedure, while PSPS-knockoff utilizes the ML-assisted debiased Lasso coefficient (Javanmard and Montanari, 2014; Zhang and Zhang, 2014; Liu and Rigollet, 2019) , in a ranking algorithm. These methods aim to improve the power of classical methods that employ linear regression or debiased Lasso (Zhang and Zhang, 2014) using only labeled data. ## 5.2 Identify vQTLs for bone mineral density

We employed our method to carry out ML-assisted quantile regression to identify genetic variants associated with the outcome variability (vQTL) of bone mineral density derived from dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry imaging (DXA-BMD) (Miao et al., 2022) . DXA-BMD serves as the primary diagnostic marker for osteoporosis and fracture risk (Zheng et al., 2015; Carey and Delaney, 2017) . Identifying vQTL for DXA-BMD can provide insights into the biological mechanisms underlying outcome plasticity and reveal candidate genetic variants potential gene-gene and gene-environment interactions (Wang et al., 2019; Marderstein et al., 2021; Westerman et al., 2022; Young et al., 2018; Miao and Lu, 2022) . We focused on total body DXA-BMD, which integrates measurements from multiple skeletal sites. We used data from the UK Biobank (Bycroft et al., 2018) , which includes 36,971 labeled and 319,548 unlabeled samples with 9,450,880 genetic variants after quality control. Data processing details have been reported elsewhere (Miao et al., 2024) . We predicted DXA-BMD values in both labeled and unlabeled samples using SoftImpute (Mazumder et al., 2010) with 466 other variables measured in UK Biobank. Prediction in the labeled sample was implemented through cross-validation to avoid overfitting. We used the BH procedure to correct for multiple testing and considered FDR < 0.05 as the significance threshold.
No genetic variants reached statistical significance under the classical method with only labeled data. PSPS identified 108 significant variants with FDR < 0.05 spanning 5 independent loci, showcasing the superior statistical power of PSPS (fig. 5 and table 1 ). # 6 Conclusion

In this work, we introduced a simple, task-agnostic protocol for ML-assisted inference, with applications across a broad range of statistical tasks. We established consistency and asymptotic normality of the proposed estimator. Additionally, we introduce several extensions to further expand the scope of our approach, addressing challenges such as dependence and distributional shifts between labeled and labeled data, and FDR control. Through extensive simulations and applications to real data, we demonstrate the superior performance and broad applicability of our method across diverse tasks. As a future direction, it would be interesting to develop and implement a fast algorithm for resampling in our method.
The codes to implement PSPS, along with the benchmark methods, and to replicate the simulation and real data analysis, are available at https://github.com/qlu-lab/PSPS.
